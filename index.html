New Owner's Guide 2026

Side by Side Accessories

The complete SxS accessory checklist — everything you need before your first trail run, ranked by priority and honest buying advice

Last updated: June 26, 2026 | Written for new SxS owners and experienced riders expanding their setup

You just bought a side-by-side — congratulations. The machine is ready to ride right off the dealer lot. You might not be. Every SxS that rolls out of the factory is built to a price point, which means certain accessories that dramatically improve safety, comfort, and capability were left out to hit the retail target. This guide tells you exactly what to add, in what order, and why — so your first trail run is safe, fun, and leaves you wanting more instead of stranded in the backcountry.

Priority Order: What Every New SxS Owner Needs

Every accessory on this list has been prioritized by real-world importance: safety first, capability second, comfort third, luxury last. Don't skip to comfort accessories before addressing safety — that's how otherwise good riding days go wrong.

🔴 Phase 1 — Safety Non-Negotiables (Add Before Riding)

  • Helmet (full-face recommended): Required at most OHV parks, mandatory for common sense. Full-face protects your jaw and face from debris.
  • Recovery strap (30 ft minimum): Every trail ride risks a stuck vehicle. A quality tow or kinetic strap gets you out without a tow truck.
  • Tire plug kit + mini compressor: Trail punctures are common. A plug kit and mini compressor get you back rolling in 10 minutes vs waiting hours for help.
  • First aid kit (compact): Cuts, abrasions, and minor injuries happen. A compact trail first aid kit handles most trail medicine situations adequately.
  • Communication device: Phone works where there's signal. A GMRS radio or satellite communicator covers you when cell service disappears.

🟡 Phase 2 — Capability Essentials (Add Early)

  • LED light bar: Extends riding hours into early morning and evening. Dramatically improves safety in dust and low visibility.
  • Storage system (tunnel bag + door bags): The factory storage is minimal. A proper bag system means you can actually carry what you need.
  • Limit straps (if hard riding): Critical safety item for any lifted SxS or aggressive trail riding. Protects CV axles and suspension from overdroop damage.
  • Skid plates: Rocky terrain requires undercarriage protection. Stock protection is minimal on most SxS platforms.

🟢 Phase 3 — Comfort Upgrades (Add as Budget Allows)

  • Half windshield or full windshield: Wind blast reduction is the single most impactful comfort upgrade on open-cab SxS platforms.
  • Sun shade (bimini or hard top): Essential for desert, dunes, and summer mountain riding. Reduces cockpit temperature 15-25°F.
  • Aftermarket seats or seat foam upgrade: Reduces fatigue on all-day rides. Even a $60 foam kit dramatically improves long-ride comfort.
  • Seat covers: Protect OEM seats, manage heat, and improve appearance. Quality seat covers from Bartact or similar are a long-term investment in your SxS interior.

The Accessories That Are Overrated for New Riders

The SxS accessory market is full of products that look great in product photos but don't deliver meaningful real-world improvement. For new owners especially, avoid these common overspend traps:

  • Carbon fiber trim pieces: Look good in photos, add no performance or capability. Skip until you've covered actual capability needs.
  • Ultra-premium audio systems: A $1,200 audio system in an open-cab SxS sounds like a $300 system at riding speeds over 30 mph. Audio is best addressed after enclosing the cab.
  • Oversized light bars: A 50-inch bar on a narrow trail SxS is harder to mount, harder to aim, and more likely to catch on obstacles. A 30-inch bar is the sweet spot for most SxS applications.
  • Winch before recovery straps: A winch is useful, but a good recovery strap system ($80) handles 80% of common stuck situations faster and cheaper. Build your strap kit first.
  • Decorative wheel spacers: Properly engineered wheel spacers serve a purpose. Cheap decorative spacers introduce stress concentrations that can cause hub failure under load — not worth the risk.

  • Duct tape

Frequently Asked Questions

What accessories do I need before my first side-by-side trail ride?

Before your first trail ride: recovery strap, tire plug kit + compressor, first aid kit, communication device (radio or satellite), and a proper helmet. These are non-negotiable for any trail ride. Everything else is an enhancement.

How much should I budget for side-by-side accessories?

A reasonable first-year accessory budget is $500–$1,500 depending on riding style. Safety and recovery essentials run $150–$300. Comfort accessories (windshield, shade, seat covers) add $200–$500. Storage and organization add $100–$300.

What is the most important safety accessory for a side-by-side?

A properly fitted full-face helmet is the single most critical safety accessory. After personal protection, recovery gear (tow strap, tire kit) is the next priority for any trail rider.

Do I need aftermarket seat covers for my side-by-side?

Not immediately, but quality seat covers improve long-ride comfort and protect OEM seats from UV damage. In hot climates, light-colored covers significantly reduce seat surface temperature — a meaningful comfort improvement on all-day rides.

What tires should I buy for trail riding?

For general trail riding, an all-terrain tire with moderate tread (Maxxis Carnivore, ITP Cryptid) balances trail grip with reasonable speed capability. Factory tires are adequate for casual trail use before committing to specialty rubber.

Do I need a winch on my side-by-side?

A winch is valuable for solo riders or remote terrain. For group riding where other UTVs provide strap recovery, a winch is less critical. Consider a 3,000–4,500 lb winch for most SxS platforms if you ride solo.

What communication gear do I need for group riding?

GMRS radios are the current standard for UTV group communication. Choose 5W+ power units for adequate range in hilly terrain. At least one radio per vehicle is the baseline for safe group riding communication.

Are helmets required to ride a side-by-side?

Requirements vary by state and trail system — many OHV parks require helmets for all riders. Even where not required, a quality full-face helmet is strongly recommended. SxS rollover crashes are far more survivable with proper head protection.

What side-by-side accessories are worth buying used?

Storage bags, cargo nets, windshields, and light bars can be purchased used with minimal risk. Never buy used helmets, harnesses, or roll cage components — safety gear integrity cannot be verified secondhand.

What's the best first upgrade for a new side-by-side?

An LED light bar is the single highest-impact upgrade for most new owners — it extends riding hours, improves safety in dust and low visibility, and is straightforward to install on any SxS cage.

How do I choose the right accessories for my specific SxS brand?

Most accessories are model-specific. A Polaris RZR windshield won't fit a Can-Am X3. Always verify fitment for your specific brand, model, and year. Reputable brands list compatible models explicitly in product descriptions.

What should I pack for a trail ride?

Day ride essentials: tire plug kit, mini compressor, first aid kit, tow strap, basic tools, 2L+ water per person, food, rain layer, and a communication device. Pack in a tunnel bag organized by category for quick access.
